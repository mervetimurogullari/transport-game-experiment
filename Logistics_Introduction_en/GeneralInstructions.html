In the transport sector, companies can often save money (and reduce CO₂ emissions) by collaborating in the transport of cargo. However, such collaboration requires negotiation between companies on who will collaborate with who and on how to divide the earnings of the collaboration. We study such negotiations in this experiment.

You and two other participants each represent a different transport company. The three companies transport a total of 9 tons of cargo each day from Amsterdam to Paris. Each company uses a truck sized to its own freight needs.

  • Company A: The market leader, transporting 4 tons of cargo every day.
  • Company B: A medium-sized company, transporting 3 tons of cargo every day.
  • Company C: A smaller company, transporting 2 tons of cargo every day.

A larger truck is available that can carry up to 9 tons. This truck, however, is only allowed on the road if it carries at least 5 tons. This means that none of the companies individually have enough cargo to use this larger truck, they need to form a coalition to do so.

A very attractive proposal has arisen. There is a company in Paris that offers a 9000-euro premium if the large truck is brought to Paris. This makes a coalition among companies to share the large truck more attractive.
